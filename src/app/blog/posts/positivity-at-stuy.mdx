## Positivity

High school is stressful. Mental health concerns are often overlooked, gradually fostering an environment that lacks positivity. Peers often joke about harming themselves, to the point where it is difficult to distinguish humor from distress. That normalization disturbs me deeply. I’ve learned to become a naturally optimistic person, both as a defense mechanism and a conscious choice. Choosing to see the good in people and situations has genuinely improved my wellbeing, and I wanted others to experience how life-changing that mindset can be. This was the wake up call that told me how exactly I could help others see the benefits of a positive and optimistic mindset. 

***
## Three Seconds

<b>November 22</b>, I was scrolling through Instagram when I came across a reel from another school asking students, “Tell me something good that happened to you today.” I instantly wanted to recreate this at my high school, so I told some of my friends in a club group chat that `i wanna do this`, only to retract my statement seconds later and change it to `no, im gonna do it`. 

I immediately began figuring out how to turn my plan into reality. I brainstormed logistics with friends and created a post on a club account to perhaps generate some hype and make people aware that it was happening—but more importantly, to set a date and hold myself accountable. I searched around my home and, slowly but surely, dug up a bunch of my dad's old equipment (mics, XLR cables, mic stand, audio mixer, etc). 

***
## One Cold Afternoon

On December 8, 2025, the day I had committed to, I carried everything to school and, at the end of the day, set up on the bridge outside the main entrance. From around 3 PM to almost 6 PM, in low-twenty-degree weather, I stood there inviting students to respond to a simple prompt: “<i>Tell me something good that happened today.</i>” At first, people were hesitant and walked past, unsure of what I was doing. But gradually, more students stopped, smiled, and shared genuine moments from their day. I stayed as long as I could, knowing many students had extracurriculars that ended later, and only packed up once the flow of people had nearly stopped.

When I got home, I immediately began editing—cutting out silences, applying a base LUT, and formatting/cropping the video for Instagram. Two days later, I posted the first video. I went on to repeat this process three more times with different prompts and for different occasions (such as Counselor Appreciation Week in February). 

***
## Worth it? Every moment. 

The response was overwhelming—far exceeding anything I imagined. As of March 2026, the videos have reached over 150K+ accounts and accumulated more than 270K+ views. My school’s principal even commented on my reels from his personal account three times! The first video was shown at a School Leadership Team meeting, and later played at a teachers’ conference—all of them saw it! Seeing how strongly students and teachers connected in these moments encouraged me to continue the #PositivityAtStuy series monthly until June (I had to take a break in March because of other commitments, but I'm definitely going to do something in April, and then Teachers' Appreciation Week in early May). 

> **I’ve always loved making content, and this kind of wholesome work is something I genuinely enjoy. I love spreading joy, helping others smile, and bringing light to their days, sometimes turning someone’s bad day into a better one.**

Looking back, I'm really glad I decided to push myself and keep myself accountable by setting a date by which I have to be ready to go out and do the work I told myself I would do. This project turned out to be very important to me, and it's the project that I've been looking to do for ages, but never knew what. 

***
## Context

In the fall semester, I took an English class called Writing To Make Change, and part of that class helped me think of ways to contribute to society and possibly have an effect on people. It turns out that this very class was the catalyst for some of the best actions I have ever taken in my life. What stood out to me most was how quickly I made up my mind. I initially shared the reel I saw with some of my friends, mentioning that I wanted to do it at my school. This class, however, gave me the courage to move from wanting to do it to deciding to actually do it. All it took was three seconds—literally. And through the class and this project, they helped break the illusion I once believed: that change is hard to come by and difficult to initiate.

***
## Takeaways

I think the biggest takeaway from this class and project is the willpower to press the “send” button or the “upload” button. It’s deciding in the moment that if I’m going to go forward with a project, or respond to an article I read, I will commit and carry out the work I’ve decided to do. The class involved/required sending emails and/or making calls to representatives and important people, and I would have never committed to sending out my first Letter to the Editor if I hadn’t taken this class. I would have never decided in the moment to bring bulky equipment to school and stand on a bridge for several hours in freezing temperatures just to make people happy. If I had never taken this course, I wouldn’t have given a second thought to even the idea of making change. Despite not wanting to be a bystander, I would have been one in the end—a hypocrite.

> **At the end of the day, I sleep happy, knowing that I’ve made a meaningful impact on my community.**

Taking this course and ultimately coming up with #PositivitiyAtStuy has helped me understand that making change starts with changing myself and holding myself accountable to my words and carrying out the actions I say I will take. Once I did that, everything else followed.
